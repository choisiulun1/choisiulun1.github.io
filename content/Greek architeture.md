- Greek architecture is renowned for its iconic columns and capitals, featuring fluted shafts and intricately designed capitals at the top. Symmetry and carefully calculated proportions played a significant role in Greek architectural design, emphasizing balance and harmony. Ornate sculpture adorned many Greek buildings, including friezes and metopes that depicted scenes from mythology and daily life. The three distinct orders of Greek architecture—Doric, Ionic, and Corinthian—dictated the design of columns, detailing, and proportions.

- The periodization of Greek architecture can be divided into the Doric, Ionic, and Corinthian periods. Doric period (7th - 5th century BC) featured simple, robust structures with rectangular columns without bases. The Ionic period (6th - 4th century BC) introduced taller, slenderer columns with volute capitals and a greater use of ornamentation. The Corinthian period (4th century BC onwards) is characterized by elaborate capitals adorned with acanthus leaves, often seen in temples and buildings of higher status.
